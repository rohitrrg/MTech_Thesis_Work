# 2.1. Related Work

Our research builds upon two major pillars of deep learning and scientific computing: data-driven weather prediction and model optimization.

**Data-Driven Weather Prediction:** The paradigm of using deep learning for weather forecasting has gained significant traction, challenging the long-standing dominance of purely physics-based Numerical Weather Prediction (NWP) models. Keisler (2022) demonstrated that graph neural networks could produce skillful forecasts, while Pathak et al. (2022) introduced FourCastNet, the foundation of our work, which used a vision transformer-based architecture (the AFNO) to achieve state-of-the-art performance. Other notable contributions include Google DeepMind's GraphCast (Lam et al., 2022) and Pangu-Weather (Bi et al., 2023), which have further pushed the boundaries of forecast accuracy and lead time. These models have established that deep learning can not only match but in some cases exceed the skill of traditional models at a fraction of the computational cost.

**Model Compression and Optimization:** The drive for efficiency in deep learning is not new. Han et al. (2015) pioneered early work on network pruning, demonstrating that significant redundancy exists in large neural networks. More recently, techniques have evolved to include structured pruning, which removes entire filters or layers for more hardware-friendly speedups (Li et al., 2016). Quantization, the process of reducing the numerical precision of model weights, has also become a standard tool, with works like Jacob et al. (2018) establishing the viability of 8-bit and lower-precision inference. Our work applies these established optimization techniques specifically to the domain of weather forecasting, providing a systematic study of the trade-offs involved.
